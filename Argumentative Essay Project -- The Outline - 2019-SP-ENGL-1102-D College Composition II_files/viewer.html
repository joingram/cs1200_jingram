Argumentative Project -- The Outline
After completing your proposal and annotated bibliography, you will submit your argumentative
essay
sentence
outline. Format your outline as follows.
Argumentative Essay Outline
I.
Introduction
A.
Exordium
: These beginning or opening words (the so-called “hook”) are
designed to introduce the issue in an interesting way and win the attention and
good will of the reader. (Begin with a short narrative, an anecdote, a question, a
quotation, an unusual, shocking, or interesting fact or statistic.)
B.
Exposition or Narration
: Include information on what gave rise to the present
problem or how the issue developed. Explain the current status of the situation,
why the subject is meaningful, why the topic is so controversial. What are the
positions of the interested parties/authorities on the issue? Present the opposing
viewpoints in an unbiased way. (
Note:
At this point, you are primarily setting the
context and reviewing the current status of the issue as well as identifying the
opposing viewpoints. A more extensive history of the issue should occur in the
body of essay.
C.
Proposition (the Thesis)
: State clearly the position you are taking. What do you
want to prove? What is the proposition you are trying to support or defend?
D.
Division of Proofs (the Plan of Development)
: Briefly lay out the main
arguments (the proofs, the reasons) that you will be using to support your
position. List these in the same order that you will address them in the body of
the essay. Usually the strongest, most important argument will be listed first here
and then discussed first in the body of the essay.
II.
Body of the Argument Essay
A. History of the Issue:
Provide here a brief overview of any needed background
information on the issue, a short summary of the history of the controversy.
(Remember, the focus here is on the history of the conflict, what led up to the current
logger jam, not the whole history of the topic from the beginning of time.)
B.
Confirmation:
In this section, present your reasons, your arguments, your
evidence for holding this position on the disputed issue. Pay attention to the order
in which you discuss these points. What follows is just one plan; there are other
options.
1.
Reason #1: Start with your best argument. (State the point clearly in a
complete sentence.)
a.
Discuss this first reason—explain why you hold this view.
b.
Evidence
: What evidence do you have to support this point--facts, relevant
statistics, examples, opinions supported by facts, reports, surveys,
testimony of experts/authorities, precedent, analogy, comparisons?
c.
Discussion:
How does this research support your position?
2.
Reason #2: Present your next best argument in support of your position.
a.
Discuss this second point—explain why you hold this view.
b.
Evidence:
What evidence do you have to support this point--facts,
relevant statistics, examples, opinions supported by facts, reports, surveys,
testimony of experts/authorities, precedent, analogy, comparisons?
c.
Discussion
: How does this research support your position?
3.
Reason #3: Present your next best argument. (Alternatively, consider
discussing your weakest argument here. Some scholars advise discussing the
weakest argument in the middle of essay, which means you begin and end
with strong arguments.)
a.
Again, discuss this point. Why do you think this point is valid?
b.
Evidence:
What evidence do you have to support this point--facts,
relevant statistics, examples, opinions supported by facts, reports, surveys,
testimony of experts/authorities, precedent, analogy, comparisons?
c.
Discussion
: How does this research support your position?
4.
Reason #4: Present another argument (or your final argument).
a.
Discuss the point. Explain how this point supports your thesis.
b.
Evidence:
What evidence do you have to support this point--facts,
relevant statistics, examples, opinions supported by facts, reports, surveys,
testimony of experts/authorities, precedent, analogy, comparisons?
c.
Discussion
: How does this research support your position?
C.
Refutation:
In this section of the essay, acknowledge the opposition viewpoints
and present your counterarguments. Be polite. It is wise—and a good strategy--
to admit that some opposition views may have merit, but obviously you believe
that the arguments you have made are the stronger and the more reasonable.
1.
Opposing view #1— address one opposition argument.
a.
Discuss this first point—explain why your opponents hold this view.
b.
Evidence:
What evidence does the opposition have to support this point--
facts, relevant statistics, examples, opinions supported by facts, reports,
surveys, testimony of experts/authorities, precedent, analogy,
comparisons?
c.
Discussion
: How does this research support the opposition position?
2.
Opposing view #2— address another opposition argument.
(Organize info in paragraph as shown above—include evidence.)
3.
Opposing view #3— address another opposition argument.
(Organize info in paragraph as shown above—include evidence.)
4.
Counterarguments
— Refute each opposing viewpoint above one by one and
in order. Demonstrate that each opposing viewpoint is seriously flawed in
some way. (Perhaps the claim does not make logical sense, is not founded on
fact or not supported by the evidence. Perhaps it is exaggerated, ambiguous,
or otherwise unsound.)
III. Conclusion:
restate your opinion, summarize important points, propose a future
course of action, or suggest possible solutions to the problem.
